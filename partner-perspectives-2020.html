Volatility

Eagle Equity Income Team

With continued trade uncertainty, slow global economic growth, and the U.S. heading into a presidential election year, do you see headline volatility remaining elevated in 2020? Are there any specific sectors/industries or investment themes that you believe could benefit in this environment? How, if at all, do you position around this?

Market participants have always had to deal with headline volatility. We think it may be a psychological phenomenon that we look back and pine for the days that were simpler and calmer. But if we are honest with ourselves, there has never been a time without turmoil. Clearly, we have been in an extended period where the old adage “the market climbs a wall of worry” has reigned. We like to say that this is the most unloved bull market in history.

If we look at volatility mathematically, by analyzing movements in the CBOE Market Volatility Index (VIX), we find that volatility in 2019 is slightly below the average for the last 10 years. We understand the limitations of looking at the average, as it is really the spikes in the index that cause angst in the market. Looking at it visually, it is clear we are not in the extremely elevated volatility market of 2009, nor are we in the depressed volatility market of 2017. Just about average— even though it doesn’t feel that way.

Our strategy seeks to reduce volatility in our clients’ portfolios in all types of market scenarios. To achieve our goals, we strive to create a diversified portfolio across industries. While we constantly search for the best risk vs. reward outcome for our clients, which may result in over- and under-weighted industries, our focus is on maintaining a balanced portfolio of high-quality stocks.

We do expect volatile political and trade headlines in 2020 and beyond. Our current view is that the economic expansion in the United States will continue into at least 2021. Bolstering this view is the new easier attitude at the Federal Reserve (Fed), which promulgated three recent interest rate cuts, a marked reversal from the ill-timed interest rate hikes in September and December 2018.

We have exposure to economically sensitive stocks, with a preference for companies that have strong pricing power and solid competitive advantages, such as selected software, semiconductor, and industrial names. Many software names are growing rapidly due to a shift toward cloud computing, and selected semiconductors are expected to benefit from the potential growth of 5G wireless networks, the Internet of Things (IoT), artificial intelligence, and more electronics in motor vehicles. We view these long-term trends as less subservient to trade outcomes. We own selected, innovative industrial companies levered to the treatment of drinking and wastewater; growth in composite materials in aircraft; prioritized areas of the defense budget; auto auctions; and increased use of data services and consulting. Our healthcare companies help treat genetic disorders, and provide medical devices for cardiac or urological care for an aging population. Cruising remains a popular and growing leisure activity with better prospects due to more accommodative monetary policy and favorable fuel prices.

Trade War/Tariffs

Reams Asset Management

What macroeconomic factors should investors be watching and why?

It is clear that global growth has slowed due to a variety of factors. The U.S.-China trade war and lingering Brexit overhang, despite some recent improvements on both fronts, are the most obvious proximal causes. We also believe the impact of the Fed’s tightening cycle was not fully appreciated while it was ongoing, as the effects were being somewhat masked and offset by fiscal stimulus stemming from the 2017 tax cuts.

As the fourth quarter of 2018 began, the Fed was contemplating more rate increases and balance sheet reduction was on “autopilot” despite signs that growth and inflation were already decelerating. The abrupt policy shift and subsequent “mid-cycle adjustment” of three 25 bps rate cuts have blunted the impact of the slowdown and reduced the likelihood of a U.S recession. Europe is still in a perilous state, however, as Germany, France, and Italy are all flirting with recessions. China has also been employing all manner of fiscal and monetary stimulus to keep its growth rate above 6%, but continues to print disappointing economic numbers.

The key question is: Can the U.S. consumer hold up long enough for the manufacturing sector to recover? U.S. and Eurozone manufacturing PMIs have both weakened notably and are now below 50, indicating contraction, while China’s PMI has been (suspiciously) hovering around 50 for the past year with little variability. Global services PMIs, which are less exposed to the dual threats of the U.S.-China trade war and Brexit, have proven more resilient and remain in expansion territory. The U.S. consumer, buoyed by historically low unemployment, has been one of the only bright spots globally.

The overriding hope is that a comprehensive trade deal gets done before the consumer rolls over and potentially tips the U.S. into a recession. There are a couple of issues, however, with this otherwise bullish scenario. First, although market participants are concerned about the U.S.-China trade war, some sort of favorable resolution appears to be largely priced in at this point. This is similar to the situation earlier in 2019, when a breakdown in talks between the U.S. and China roiled markets in May. The reaction to these developments made it clear that the consensus belief was that a deal was inevitable, and markets seem to believe that to be the case once again.

Second, a quick fix to this issue may not actually exist. “Phase one” of a trade deal is expected to be signed in early January, although certain details have yet to be released. In the main, additional tariffs set to go into effect on December 15 were avoided, the U.S. agreed to reduce tariffs on $120 billion of Chinese goods, and China agreed to import more U.S. goods, primarily agricultural products. This is not a comprehensive trade deal, however, and should be viewed more like a temporary armistice than a lasting peace treaty. Some of the near-term tail risk has been reduced, but there are still a number of critical long-term issues that need to be addressed.

China has been hurt more by the trade war than the U.S. has, but China has also dug in and does not seem willing to “lose” on a deal with the U.S. Similarly, the Trump administration cannot stomach a deal that appears to capitulate to China. Inking a minor deal heading into an election year turns down the heat a bit and lowers U.S. recession risk, which is certainly desirable from President Trump’s standpoint.

That is perhaps all we should expect until after the 2020 presidential election, however, and there will undoubtedly be more ups and downs along the way. The U.S. has more leverage on the actual terms of trade since it represents a much higher percentage of China’s exports than vice versa, but China has time on its side and a radically different political economy. President Xi Jinping certainly faces some internal pressures, but China can afford to wait out the U.S. election cycle before signing a comprehensive trade deal. They can bide their time either to apply more negotiating pressure as election day approaches, or with a view towards picking up talks with potentially a new incoming administration that would likely be less adversarial. So in our view, it is entirely possible that trade tensions between the two largest economies in the world will persist longer than many are projecting.

Speaking of the U.S. presidential election, this particular one is setting up to have a larger than normal impact on bond markets. Aside from President Trump winning re-election or not, a lot depends on who becomes the Democratic candidate. Barring any surprises we are likely to have a status-quo moderate, but there is potential for a progressive candidate in either Elizabeth Warren or Bernie Sanders. If Warren or Sanders is the Democratic nominee, we would expect heightened market volatility leading up to the election. Either of these candidates would be viewed as broadly negative for business and markets (in contrast to a more centrist Democratic candidate), and sectors such as healthcare, financials, and energy could suffer disproportionately based on the two progressive candidates’ likely policy agendas, if elected.

Should Warren or Sanders win, we can also expect big changes on the policy front or attempted changes, that is. A Republican-majority Senate would do whatever it can to block any sweeping reforms, leading to more incremental changes via executive order. We don’t spend much time trying to predict these sorts of things or laying out multiple branching scenarios that might unfold. But the one big-picture item that we would be closely monitoring, in the event that Trump or a more moderate Democrat is not at the helm, is some sort of massive fiscal spending bill. The U.S. is already running a $1 trillion annual budget deficit, so any large-scale fiscal package like the Green New Deal or Medicare-for-All would add substantially to the deficit and potentially cause ripple effects in the bond and currency markets.

Trade War/Tariffs

Scout International Equity Team

If we make meaningful progress on resolving trade disputes, are there specific regions or sectors/industries that you believe could benefit the most? How, if at all, do you position around that?

By now, we think most people in the U.S. don’t believe that trade wars are good and easy to win. If tariffs were described as taxes on the general public, they would have even less support. But this is what they are, especially on goods and intermediate products that cannot be substituted and have to be imported at higher prices. The manufacture of these goods migrated outside the U.S. years ago because they were more efficiently produced elsewhere. And while there may be some good news from the domestic steel industry, for example, which gets higher prices for its end products, U.S. consumers are ultimately paying those higher prices. Meanwhile, many smaller U.S. manufacturing companies are being crushed by huge cost increases on specialized products they can only get from places like China.

But this argument hasn’t stopped the U.S. from forging ahead with tariffs against China and other countries. China has long been guilty of unfair practices, including theft of intellectual property, but tariffs are a blunt instrument, and China is willing to play the long game, partly because it has no voters to appease and partly because Donald Trump will likely want to settle and declare some kind of a victory coming into his re-election campaign.

Some of the countries most impacted by the U.S.-China trade war are export-led countries that sell components in China that are included in onward trade to the U.S. According to a recent Reuters poll, half of Japanese companies have seen their profits hurt by this trade war. China also plays a huge role in the global automotive supply chain; many components that appear in U.S.- made cars come from China. Technology is another industry at the forefront of the U.S. tariff policy as many U.S. semiconductor chip makers and electronics manufacturers depend on China for sales, and the U.S. has also highlighted national security concerns with some Chinese companies. The agricultural industry in the U.S. has also been hugely impacted. China is the largest importer of U.S. soybeans and imposed retaliatory tariffs on these imports to the detriment of U.S. farmers. The sooner a comprehensive agreement can be reached, the sooner these industries can return to some kind of stability.

Eagle Vermont Investment Team

Why do you think small caps have underperformed larger caps on a relative basis since the drawdown in the fourth quarter of 2018, and what could be the catalyst to lead to outperformance?

Jason Wulff: Very simply, it’s fundamentals. 2019 earnings for the S&P SmallCap 600 Index are down 8.5%, while the S&P 500 is only down a little over 2%. A lot of it has been driven by the more cyclical sectors: energy, industrials, materials. Those areas see significantly steeper declines in terms of earnings estimates amongst smaller caps and large caps. This is not atypical; the business models tend to be more sensitive to changes in economic trends. We have seen, particularly with trade tensions and changing supply chains, that small-cap companies have been at a disadvantage in terms of sourcing from different places and having that negotiating leverage. Secondarily, we talk about investor positioning, generally toward what are perceived as higher quality, lower beta, more defensive issues, and traditionally that has been larger cap. In this market cycle, we’ve seen factor investing and stylistic investing accelerating and becoming a more meaningful driver of short-term results. That has had a bias toward the large cap.

Matthew McGeary: Certainly I think better expected earnings growth in 2020 should help close the gap a bit between large and small caps. Since September, we saw a bit of a market rotation into more value-oriented names with the expectation that we’ll see better earnings growth in 2020. That more risk-on behavior, at least in the equity market, should benefit small caps. To the extent that we get resolution on trade issues, that should also help small caps and be particularly advantageous to those more value and cyclically oriented companies.

2020 Elections

Cougar Global Investments

How do you expect the current U.S. political climate — impeachment, the Presidential and Congressional elections, healthcare policy debates — to affect the economy and financial markets?

The outcome of the 2020 Presidential and Congressional elections will have a very substantial impact on U.S. domestic and foreign policy for years to come. The impact on economic policy — and financial markets — will also be significant. Candidates for the Democratic nomination for president hold very different — sometimes diametrically opposite — views on key hot-button issues when compared to President Donald Trump. Democratic candidates are generally in favor of more regulation, higher taxes, and greater government involvement (particularly in healthcare), while Republicans are generally in favor of less regulation and fewer government programs (notwithstanding the fact that the annual government deficit now stands at over $1 trillion under President Trump). At Cougar Global, we expect President Trump to be re-elected in 2020, given the low unemployment rate and strong state of the economy. In this case, we are likely to see a continued regime of low taxes and regulations — a positive for stocks. However, there is a significant chance that — given the enormous wealth inequality in the U.S. (according to Forbes,1 the top three wealthiest individuals are worth more than the bottom 50% of the population) — voters could elect a left-leaning Democrat as president. This outcome would be less favorable for global growth and stocks, particularly for highly profitable healthcare companies. Several Democratic candidates have outlined “Medicare-for-All” initiatives that would curtail the profitability of private insurers and drug companies.

While a lot of airtime has been devoted to President Trump’s impeachment, there are no signs yet of an impact on his overall job approval ratings (as measured by Real Clear Politics) or support within the Republican Party (which stands at over 90%, according to a recent Gallup poll). Given that Democrats are unlikely to convince enough (if any) Republicans in the Senate to remove President Trump from office, the impeachment at this point is largely political theater. In our view, voters, not Congress, will decide in 2020 whether President Trump or a Democrat will occupy the White House for the next four years. On the latter, former Vice President Joe Biden, Senator Elizabeth Warren, Senator Bernie Sanders, Mayor Pete Buttigieg, and former New York Mayor Mike Bloomberg all seem to be contenders.

Federal Reserve

Reams Asset Management

What is your assessment of global monetary policy?

Monetary policy has been largely ineffective, as central banks have shown limited ability to spur growth and inflation via monetary stimulus over the past 10+ years. Emergency policy measures were implemented in the midst of the Global Financial Crisis to prevent a complete collapse of the financial system. In that narrow framework, they have indeed been successful. These extremely accommodative policies have persisted for more than a decade, however, much longer than anyone would have predicted, and their ongoing efficacy is questionable at best.

The most obvious outcome of all of this liquidity sloshing around the globe has been the boosting of risk assets and the suppression of volatility. Global growth has been decent over this timeframe, but somewhat disappointing given the massive size of the stimulus, and generating inflation has proven all but impossible.

Some will make the counterfactual argument that growth would have been much worse without the stimulus, and that we may have experienced outright deflation. This is unprovable, of course, but point granted. Perhaps we would now be in year 12 of another Great Depression without #QE4ever. The root problem, however, is not that the cost of money is too dear, choking off growth; money is in fact way too cheap and no one knows what to do with all of it.

Looking ahead, central banks have fewer monetary policy levers to pull than they did in 2008. Negative yields and quantitative easing (QE) programs are pervasive, but most developed economies still face disinflation and sluggish growth. When the next cyclical downturn occurs, central banks could take risk-free rates into even deeper negative territory or add trillions more to their already-bloated balance sheets. But even if unconventional policy tools have become the norm, they are path-dependent and the starting point matters.

Additional rate cuts may provide a short-term boost to equity markets, and more QE will compress bond yields even further, but this is unlikely to boost aggregate demand and capital expenditures. We have reached the monetary policy “event horizon,” the point where historical cause-and-effect relationships start to break down. The only likely policy impetus for a surge in growth and inflation would appear to be significant fiscal stimulus, which also faces long odds given the current political situation in both the U.S. and Europe. Although we believe it would be (yet more) folly, we cannot rule out the possibility of the Fed slashing rates and dragging U.S. yields towards the same black hole in which Japan and Europe are currently trapped.

After a relatively brief period of rising interest rates, the Fed has now shifted to a more accommodative stance, cutting the Fed funds rate several times and resuming balance sheet expansion. What kind of stimulus could this provide to the economy and capital markets?

Scout Mid Cap Equity Team

Many of the traditional “value” sectors of the market are populated with industries that can be negatively impacted by lower interest rates (banks, insurance, financials); trade uncertainties and macroeconomic weakness (metals, chemicals, automotive, energy); and political changes (energy, HMOs, pharmaceuticals, financials). We believe the distinction between “value” and “growth” stocks can be an artificial one, often a matter of subjective opinion. We have sometimes perceived that certain “growth” stocks are actually more attractively valued in our opinion than so-called “value” stocks. In a global economy where economic growth is decelerating and can be scarce due to structural issues of aging demographics, high debt levels, and relatively high domestic interest rates, companies that can grow organically can trade at a premium and perhaps have better performing stock prices than companies that lack organic volume growth or pricing power. We think meaningful trade deals, should they occur, would help lift interest rates and boost many “value” stocks, which is what happened starting in September. Longer term, we believe the more innovative and faster growing corners of the market often retain an advantage for performance due to superior competitive dynamics and business models that drive pricing power and volume growth. With weak pricing power in many “value” industries and persistently low interest rates, some “value” stocks may continue to struggle unless there is a persistent change in the global economy.

Growth vs. Value

Eagle Core Team

We saw a continuation in the significant outperformance of growth vs. value throughout most of 2019, with a sharp reversal in September. Discuss your thoughts on the large performance gap between growth and value sectors and any forces you think could impact this going forward, particularly in the in the small and small/mid cap space.

The yield curve “un-inverted” after the end of the third quarter. All other things equal, this is a positive for value stocks, although most factors continue to favor growth. If the Federal Reserve continues to cut rates, the risk of inflation is judged to be higher. Some inflation can be beneficial for cyclical value stocks over longer duration growth stocks.

In our view, three main drivers have rewarded growth stocks:

1. The relative scarcity of growth: Growth has been hard to come by, so sustainable growth stocks become the scarce asset and relatively more valuable. Pre-2008 real GDP growth after the 1999-2000 recession was 2.7%. Real GDP growth since 2010 (post the Great Recession) has been 1.7%. Numerous reasons have been given for slow growth this past decade such as declining productivity, debt hangovers, and the effects of China’s massive capital over-investment for a decade and a half.

2. Change in the margin growth pecking order: The large-cap investment universe has changed drastically since 2000. The high-margin firms are also the high-growth firms, largely due to the rise of technology monopolies. To make the top quintile of large firms in terms of free cash flow margins in 1998, a firm needed to be at the 10% level. In 2019, a top quintile ranking requires a 20% free cash flow margin. This is a key point and ties into the slow growth issue: In a world of slow growth and low rates, free cash flow margins of 20% compounding at even mid-single digits is a mechanism for enormous wealth creation. Barring anti-trust moves, our forecast shows little to stop this phenomenon. Economic recoveries and a trade deal may support short-term value recoveries but a long-term value run may require a collapse in the cycle and its ensuing recovery.

3. Low real rates favor long-duration assets: Real interest rates (nominal rates minus inflation expectations) are at all-time lows relative to history. Long-duration assets such as growth stocks are very valuable with these low discount rates. The bigger question might be: What type of world gives us these century-low rates? It suggests massive past capital over-investment and idle capacity relative to demand, a thought which is in the back of all of our minds.

While the landscape looks better for value and small-cap stocks with the un-inversion of the yield curve, we believe growth stocks will continue to show strength.
